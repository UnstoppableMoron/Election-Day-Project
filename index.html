Introduction

Presidential Election Day in the United States is a major civic event held every four years. It marks a time when Americans exercise their democratic right to vote for the President, reflecting the principles of democracy.

History

The specific date of election days used to vary by state, but a federal law was passed in 1845 to set a single election day for the entire country. It was decided that early November would be a good time, because the country's mostly agrarian population would have just finished their harvest, and the weather would be mostly mild. Instead of having elections on a predetermined date, congress decided on a specific day of the week, two of which were already off the table. Most Americans at the time were devout Christians, and thus set aside Sunday as a day of rest and worship, and Wednesday in many areas was a market day, when farmers sold their crops in town. It was also agreed that a travel day was necessary, because the nearest polling location was likely miles away for people living in rural areas, and since cars weren't around yet, it could take a while to get there. This ruled out Monday and Thursday, as they couldn't be used as travel days due to the aforementioned Sunday and Wednesday being unavailable. This left Tuesday to be percieved as the best option. It was agreed that election day would be held on the first Tuesday after the first Monday of November. The "after the first Monday" specification was added to prevent the election from falling on November 1st, which Christians saw as All Saints' Day.

Although Tuesday was originally chosen for convenience, it is now seen as more of a hassle than anything. Today, less than 2% of Americans work in agriculture, and many people work on Tuesdays. Eventually, people began to propose that election day be moved to the weekend, or become a federal holiday. This hasn't happened as of yet, although it has also become possible for people to vote online or by mail, making the voting process significantly easier.

How Voting Works

Americans can vote in person at polling stations or by mail, which is done at designated polling stations placed throughout each congressional district. Each state’s votes contribute to the Electoral College, a system that ultimately determines the winner. The Electoral College consists of 538 electors, 270 of which are required to elect the President. Each candidate running for President in your State has their own group of electors, known as a slate. The slates are generally chosen by the candidate’s political party in your State, but State laws vary on how the electors are selected and their responsibilities.

Key Facts

Here are some important facts about voting in the United States:

  • Voting age in the U.S. is 18 and above.
  • Each state has different methods and requirements for registration and voting.
  • Election Day is a public holiday in some states.

Why Voting Matters

Voting is a way for citizens to have a say in their government. Each vote counts, and it’s through this collective decision-making that leaders are chosen to represent the public's values and interests.
